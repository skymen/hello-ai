Global Climate Summit Reaches Historic Agreement
By Sarah Martinez, Climate Correspondent | June 15, 2025

PARIS - World leaders from 195 countries concluded the most ambitious climate summit in history today, reaching unprecedented agreements that could reshape the global response to climate change over the next decade.

The landmark agreement, dubbed the "Paris Accelerated Framework," commits participating nations to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 60% by 2030, far exceeding previous targets. The deal includes $2.5 trillion in funding commitments for renewable energy infrastructure and climate adaptation measures.

Key provisions of the agreement include:

  • Mandatory phase-out of coal power plants by 2028 in developed nations
  • $500 billion annual fund for developing countries' green transition
  • Global carbon tax framework starting at $50 per ton
  • Binding targets for renewable energy adoption (80% by 2030)

The United States, European Union, and China have all committed to leading by example, with each pledging to achieve net-zero emissions by 2028, two years ahead of the global target.

Environmental groups have cautiously welcomed the agreement, though some critics argue the timeline remains too aggressive for practical implementation.

"While ambitious, these targets represent what science tells us is necessary to avoid catastrophic climate change," said Dr. Sarah Chen, lead climate scientist at the International Panel on Climate Change.
